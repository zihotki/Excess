Extend programming languages

Programming languages are measured largely by their expressiveness. If the problem you are trying to solve deals with apples, a programming language made of oranges will add unnecessary complexity. Historically, language makers deal with this problem by adding as many fruits to their language as humanly possible. Which still fails when you a get a problem related to vegetables. Vegetable people are forced then to put together a bunch of fruits and try to make it look like veggies. These are called libraries, which are forced to sacrifice a lot of expressiveness because of the strong smell of fruit imposed by the owners of the language. Some others have it worse, because their problems are related to spaceships and there is no way of making one of those out of fruit, unless millions upon millions of fruit are used.

Silly produce analogies aside, every problem domain is different and those domains usually carry their own lingo, which is usually difficult to abstract in any programming language. Particularly when you would like to use an established language, full of documentation and thousands of libraries at your disposal. Such languages are outside of the control of developers and new features tend to take years to be implemented. It is not practical to write new programming languages for these domains, either. For starters, it is a very hard task to write a language; and then you loose all the goodness (tools, docs, libs).

The solution is something that looks a lot like Excess. Instead of creating new languages (even when they run on top of JVM or CLR), developers can modify their actual language of choice. Or C#, in this case. Why C#? It just offers the best tools, but the engine is written with any language in mind. There are many advantages to this approach aside of expressiveness and convenience. It turns programming languages into a community affair, with a much faster pace of innovation (no waiting years for a committee to decide). It helps standardize domain specific languages: once the community (the actual community working a particular domain) agrees on a syntax, any vendor can implement it. It just becomes a faster, more democratic process.
